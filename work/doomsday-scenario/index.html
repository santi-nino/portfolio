DOOMSDAY
A Scenario-Based Apocalypse & Disaster TTRPG
The end of the world,
in one sitting.

Doomsday is a personal project I've been building from scratch — a tabletop RPG rooted in over a decade of DMing and a love of the disaster movies of the '90s and early 2000s. The asteroid. The outbreak. The tornado on the horizon.

Where other games run for years, Doomsday runs for a night. Every scenario is a self-contained apocalypse, played as a one-shot and finished in one session. I'm building the rules, the worldbuilding, and the brand system simultaneously.

Designer's note
Built from scratch — rules, worldbuilding, and brand all in parallel. This page documents the brand identity system I've developed for the game so far.
"Grab the dice. We start when the lights go."

I've been a Dungeon Master for over a decade. Doomsday is what happens when that experience collides with a love of '90s disaster movies and a frustration with games that demand months-long commitments before anything interesting happens.

The core design goal: a complete, satisfying apocalypse in a single session. Every mechanic serves that constraint. Characters are built fast — archetypes and careers, not class trees. Scenarios are self-contained. The tone is civilian: ordinary people improvising, not trained heroes winning.

The Lexicon
SCENARIO — A single apocalypse, played as a one-shot in one session.
APOCALYPSE — The setting. Zombies, aliens, a T5 tornado.
ARCHETYPE — Who you are at your core. There's always a jock, always a nerd.
CAREER — What you did before the world ended.
TRAIT — The bread and butter: stats, skills, actions.
"None of us are trained for this. That's the game."

The wordmark is a single marker-lettered word — fat, fast, slightly uneven, like it was written on a box of supplies in the dark. Real felt-tip drag. Ends blunt, edges bleed, the weight wobbles. Never set in a clean font.

DOOMSDAY

When the wordmark won't fit — a die face, an app icon — the brand uses the Impact mark: a meteor falling to a curved horizon. The single most legible '90s-disaster image there is. Reads at 16px without a caption.

The Impact mark — app icon, token, die stamp, favicon.

Five hands, one voice. Each typeface maps to how the brand talks. Each one has a single job and does not leave its lane.

Traits are the bread and butter of Doomsday character creation. Some affect your stats, some give you skill proficiencies — others give you all three.

Seven colours, all sun-faded and field-worn. Notebook carries the page. Shelter grounds the dark. Bloodstain is a few flecks, never a flood. Hazard is a single drop.

Someone wrote this down because they had to. Doomsday speaks like a survivor leaving notes for the next person through the door — matter-of-fact about things that should terrify you.

Urgent
Every word earned. Nothing decorative. Say it and move.
"Grab the dice. We start when the lights go."
Weathered
Nothing is pristine. The brand has been through something.
"This page survived. Barely."
Pulpy
The disaster poster on your wall, not the literary journal.
"The tornado has a name. It's coming for the diner."
Instructional
Advising, not commanding. A survivor passing notes.
"Pick your archetype first. Trust me, it's easier."
Tactile
Ink on paper. Spray on concrete. Never abstract.
"Fold along the crease. Yes, that crease."
Civilian
Ordinary people improvising. The messiness is the point.
"None of us are trained for this. That's the game."
