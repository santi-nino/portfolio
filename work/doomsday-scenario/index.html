A Scenario-Based Apocalypse & Disaster TTRPG
The end of the world,
in one sitting.

Doomsday is a personal project I've been building from scratch — a tabletop RPG rooted in over a decade of DMing and a love of the disaster movies of the '90s and early 2000s. The asteroid. The outbreak. The tornado on the horizon.

Where other games run for years, Doomsday runs for a night. Every scenario is a self-contained apocalypse, played as a one-shot and finished in one session. I'm building the rules, the worldbuilding, and the brand system simultaneously — this page walks through all three, and ends with a working playtest.

01 // Brand
A VHS-era poster
that survived the apocalypse.

The brand has to feel like that poster on your wall: hand-lettered, blood-flecked, photocopied one too many times. Warm with recognition, then the floor drops out. Everything below — the marker wordmark, the Impact symbol, the weathered palette — exists to make a zombie one-shot and a killer-tornado campaign read as the same product line.

I built this system the way I'd build it for a client: a wordmark that can't be faked with a font, a single approved symbol for when the wordmark won't fit, a seven-colour palette that never goes glossy, and a five-typeface voice system that maps directly to how the game talks to its players.

02 // Mechanics
Built to finish
in one sitting.

Doomsday is designed against a single constraint: a complete, satisfying apocalypse in one session, no prep required between sessions because there are no further sessions. That constraint shapes every rule. Character creation takes minutes, not hours — players pick an Archetype (who they are at their core) and a Career (what they did before the world ended), which together hand them a small stack of Traits: stats, skill proficiencies, and signature actions. There's no levelling, no inventory management, no class tree. You build a person, not a build.

Tension comes from scarcity, not from a long campaign's worth of accumulated power. Every Scenario is tuned for a single dramatic arc — inciting incident, rising complication, the night's climax — and every Apocalypse (the zombies, the asteroid, the storm) is just a reskin of that same arc with different stakes and different rules text. That's what makes the format repeatable: new settings without a new engine.

1

Pick an Archetype

Who you are at your core. The Jock, the Nerd, the Slacker, the Survivalist — always recognizable, always playable in five minutes.

2

Pick a Career

What you did before the world ended. Adds Traits on top of your Archetype — a Nurse knows triage, a Mechanic can hot-wire the getaway car.

3

Drop into the Apocalypse

The table picks (or rolls) the setting. Same engine, new stakes — outbreak rules read differently than storm rules, but the dice work the same.

4

Survive the Night

One session, one arc, one ending. Scenarios are written to resolve — for better or worse — before anyone has to come back next week.

Six core stats — Vigor, Nerve, Charisma, Intellect, Dexterity, and Luck — drive every roll in the system. Archetypes, Careers, and Traits each nudge a couple of them up and a couple down — nobody's built to do everything well. It's small on purpose: easy to hold in your head while the building is on fire.
"Pick your archetype first. Trust me, it's easier."
03 // Settings
One engine.
Five ways to end the world.

Every Apocalypse is a complete, self-contained product within the same rules engine and the same brand system — meaning new settings ship faster than a traditional TTRPG line, without diluting what the game is. These are the five currently in active development, each one a nod to the disaster-movie touchstones that inspired it.

04 // Playtest
Build a survivor.

The actual character-creation flow, condensed: pick your Apocalypse, your Archetype, your Career, and two extra Traits of your choosing. Every pick carries real traits — stats up, stats down, sometimes a skill, sometimes an item. Nobody is good at everything. By the end you've got a finished character sheet, warts and all.
