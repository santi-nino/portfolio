D&D

Jalanthar

Personal project

Three years of running a homebrew D&D campaign — writing the world, managing a cast of NPCs and factions, planning sessions weeks in advance, and eventually building a custom web app to track all of it. This is what sustained creative work actually looks like.

Jalanthar is a fictional city I built from scratch and ran as a long-form D&D campaign over three years and counting. Every session required planning — not just the next encounter, but how it fit into a larger arc, how player decisions would ripple forward, and how the world would continue to feel alive whether or not the players ever went looking in a particular direction.

That kind of work builds a specific skill set: holding a lot of interdependent threads in your head, knowing which details matter and which ones can stay loose, communicating complex systems to a group of people with different levels of engagement. It's organizational thinking dressed up as storytelling.

The campaign has run 40+ sessions, generated a cast of 60+ named NPCs, and produced several hundred pages of notes, lore, and narrative documentation — none of which any player will ever see directly, but all of which makes the world feel like it exists beyond the edges of the map.

The writing side of this is its own discipline. Good D&D writing has to operate on two levels: detailed enough to feel real, sparse enough to let the players complete it themselves. An NPC needs a voice that's immediately recognizable the second time a player meets them. A faction's motivations need to be internally consistent even when they're working against each other. Backstory needs to exist so that a question you never planned for still has an answer.

Over three years, I've written tavern menus and city histories, villain monologues and merchant dialogue, political intrigue and mythological lore. The constraint that sharpens all of it: everything eventually gets tested live, in real time, in front of an audience with full license to go sideways.

About a year in, the campaign got complex enough that notes stopped being enough. I built a custom web app — the Jalanthar Player's Chronicle — to manage it. It runs as a deployed React + Firebase app with a DM-controlled backend, player-facing views with visibility controls, and an AI-powered price list scanner for in-world inventory.

Features include an interactive pan/zoom city map with custom building markers, a full NPC database with relationship graphs showing faction connections, a building catalog with type-specific inventory and service systems, and a DM login panel where I can hide or reveal information to players in real time. It's deployed to GitHub Pages via CI/CD and backed by Firestore with proper security rules — unrevealed NPCs are blocked at the database level, not just hidden in the UI.

I built it because I needed it, not because it was impressive. But it reflects how I tend to approach complex, long-running projects: find the organizational friction, remove it systematically, and build something that keeps the creative work moving.
